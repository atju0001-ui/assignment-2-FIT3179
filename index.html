According to the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD, 2023), Australia ranks 10th out of 21 countries for the proportion of people aged 15 and over living with overweight or obesity. Around 64% of Australians fall into this category, which is higher than the OECD average of 59%.

When focusing on obesity alone, Australia ranks equal fourth for men (32%), alongside Mexico and behind New Zealand (33%), Hungary (36%), and the United States (44%). Among women, Australia ranks ninth out of 22 countries, with 30% classified as obese — again above the OECD average of 26%.

Australia continues to face a significant public-health challenge from excess weight. In 2017–18, around 67% of adults and 24.9% of children were overweight or obese. High body mass accounted for 7% of the nation’s total disease and injury burden, second only to smoking. Key drivers include poor diet and low physical activity: low fruit and vegetable intake contributed 2% and 1.4% of the burden respectively, while physical inactivity contributed 5%. These risks and the prevalence of excess weight were generally higher in lower-socioeconomic areas and outside major cities, according to the National Health Survey.

Attention is increasingly turning to how the environment shapes these behaviours and outcomes. For the first time, the 2017–18 National Health Survey captured measures such as proximity to supermarkets and fast-food outlets. Using these data, this article examines how access to these outlets relates to physical activity, BMI, and consumption of fruit, vegetables, and sugar sweetened drinks. Other built environment features including access to public open space, amenities, population density, and commuting distance will be explored in future work. Analyses of supermarkets and fast-food outlets are presented separately here, noting that these venues are often located with other commercial destinations, which can affect results.
